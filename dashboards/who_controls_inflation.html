Who Actually Controls Inflation?

The post-COVID inflation spike wasn't driven by government spending — it was used cars, shelter, and energy. The Fed raised rates to fight it, but shelter inflation persisted long after used cars reversed. Here's what the data actually shows.

🚗 Used Cars — Peak YoY (Jun 2021)
+45.2%
Only 2.7% of the CPI basket — but contributed ~12% of post-COVID inflation. Now back at −3.2% YoY.
🏠 Shelter — Peak YoY (Mar 2023)
+8.2%
32% of the CPI basket. Peaked 9 months after headline CPI. Still running at 3.0% — the main reason inflation isn't back to 2%.
🏦 Fed Funds Rate — Peak (Jul–Sep 2023)
5.33%
Highest since 2001. Rate hikes could not directly fix a shelter shortage or a used-car supply chain collapse — structural problems, not demand ones.
The core tension: The Fed's primary tool — raising interest rates — reduces demand. But post-COVID inflation was largely supply-side: semiconductor shortages collapsed used car inventory (45% YoY spike), and a decade of under-building caused shelter costs to surge independently of monetary policy. Higher rates actually made the housing shortage worse by freezing homeowners into low-rate mortgages and killing new construction.
